# Proposal Structure

## Purpose

This structure keeps proposals practical, scoped, and tied to discovery. It avoids pricing, stack choices, and implementation commitments unless separately approved.

## 1. Problem

Describe the operational problem in plain business language.

Include:

- The workflow or process causing friction.
- Who is affected.
- Why the problem matters.
- What repeated manual work, risk, delay, or visibility issue exists.

Avoid:

- Tool-first framing.
- Broad AI claims.
- Unvalidated assumptions.

## 2. Current state

Summarise how the workflow works today.

Include:

- Current workflow steps.
- Systems, documents, spreadsheets, inboxes, or tools involved.
- Owners and handoff points.
- Inputs and outputs.
- Manual effort and failure points.
- Constraints and human approval requirements.

## 3. Proposed approach

Explain the practical approach to improve the workflow.

Include:

- Recommended service or service combination.
- Target workflow direction.
- Human approval points.
- Data, access, and governance considerations.
- Whether the first step is audit, workflow design, internal tool planning, knowledge system design, agentic engineering, or operational AI infrastructure planning.

Avoid:

- Pricing assumptions.
- Stack, vendor, platform, integration, or dependency decisions unless separately approved.
- Promises of full autonomy.

## 4. Deliverables

List what the client should receive.

Examples:

- Discovery summary.
- Current-state workflow map.
- Target workflow map.
- Automation opportunity list.
- Risk and access notes.
- Validation checklist.
- Handoff guide.
- Improvement backlog.

Each deliverable should map to `project-brain/11-product/DELIVERABLE_DEFINITIONS.md`.

## 5. Validation

Explain how success will be checked.

Include:

- Acceptance criteria.
- Representative examples or scenarios.
- Human approval checks.
- Documentation read-back.
- Security, access, and source-truth checks where relevant.
- What must be true before handoff.

## 6. Risks

List known risks and assumptions.

Include:

- Unclear ownership.
- Missing or messy inputs.
- Sensitive data or access constraints.
- Workflow exceptions.
- Scope uncertainty.
- Validation limitations.
- Dependencies on client availability or source material.

## 7. Out of scope

State what is not included.

Common exclusions:

- Pricing not defined in this structure.
- Stack, platform, vendor, integration, deployment, or dependency choices unless separately approved.
- Regulated legal, medical, financial, or compliance advice unless separately validated and approved.
- Fully autonomous decisions for judgment-heavy work.
- Work outside the agreed workflow.
- Reference repo modification or archive extraction unless explicitly approved.

## Proposal quality check

Before sending or using a proposal, confirm:

- It is based on discovery evidence.
- It defines deliverables and validation.
- It separates assumptions from known facts.
- It includes risks and out-of-scope items.
- It avoids unapproved pricing, stack, dependency, or compliance claims.
