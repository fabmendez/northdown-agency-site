# Discovery Process

## Purpose

Discovery turns a vague AI or automation request into a clear operational problem, decision context, and first practical next step.

## Discovery principles

- Start with the current workflow, not the tool request.
- Ask what happens before, during, and after the repeated task.
- Identify who owns decisions and who handles exceptions.
- Treat sensitive data, access, and approvals as first-class constraints.
- Avoid solution design until the workflow is understood.

## Inputs

- First-contact notes.
- Client business context.
- Known workflow pain points.
- Examples of documents, spreadsheets, forms, inbox flows, or reports where approved.
- Existing constraints, risks, and systems mentioned by the client.

## Humans do

- Lead the conversation.
- Ask follow-up questions when answers are vague.
- Judge whether the work is commercially and operationally sensible.
- Confirm what information can be shared with agents or automation.
- Decide whether the client is a fit for audit, proposal, or no further action.

## Agents do

- Structure raw notes into a discovery summary.
- Identify repeated tasks, handoffs, delays, risks, and missing owners.
- Draft clarification questions.
- Compare the problem against the service ladder.
- Flag missing assumptions before proposal work begins.

## Automation happens

- Intake responses can be sorted into standard sections.
- Repeated workflow steps can be converted into draft maps.
- Gaps and unanswered questions can be turned into follow-up lists.
- Discovery outputs can feed audit templates after human approval.

## Discovery questions

### Business context

- What does the business do?
- Which team or role is affected by the problem?
- What is the cost of leaving the process as it is?
- What has already been tried?

### Workflow detail

- What starts the workflow?
- What information is needed?
- Who does each step?
- Where does the work slow down or fail?
- What decisions require human judgment?
- What is the final output?

### Systems and information

- Where is the information stored today?
- Which tools are involved?
- What data is sensitive or restricted?
- Are there existing templates, procedures, or examples?
- What must not be changed without approval?

### Success and constraints

- What would make the workflow noticeably better?
- What would count as a failed project?
- What must be reviewed by a person?
- What deadlines, access limits, or operational constraints exist?

## Discovery deliverables

- Discovery summary.
- Current workflow outline.
- Stakeholder and owner list.
- Pain points and repeated tasks.
- Sensitive data and access notes.
- Open questions.
- Recommendation: audit, proposal, defer, or no fit.

## Stop conditions

Stop before proposal if:

- The business problem is unclear.
- The client wants automation without access, ownership, or approval boundaries.
- Sensitive data handling is unclear.
- The request requires regulated advice outside approved capability.
- The expected outcome depends on pricing, stack, or integrations that have not been approved.
