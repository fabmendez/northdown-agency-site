# Client Boundaries

## Purpose

This document defines what Northdown Computers AI Systems & Automation does, what it does not do, and which projects are good or poor fits.

## What Northdown does

Northdown helps small and mid-sized businesses improve operations through practical AI systems, automation workflows, internal tools, knowledge systems, agentic engineering, and operational AI infrastructure.

Northdown does:

- Start with discovery before recommending a solution.
- Map workflows, owners, inputs, outputs, and decision points.
- Identify useful automation opportunities and unsuitable automation ideas.
- Design workflows with human approval where judgment matters.
- Build or support internal tools only after approved scope and technical decisions.
- Create source-truth structures, project-brain docs, source-map notes, and handoff material.
- Document risks, assumptions, validation, and support boundaries.
- Keep work small, reviewable, and tied to practical business outcomes.

## What Northdown does not do

Northdown does not:

- Promise fully autonomous business operations.
- Replace human judgment for important business decisions.
- Start implementation without approved scope and change control.
- Add dependencies, integrations, infrastructure, or stack choices without approval.
- Modify reference repos or extract archives without explicit approval.
- Make pricing, timeline, service-level, or performance guarantees in planning docs.
- Claim legal, medical, financial, security compliance, or regulated advice unless separately validated and approved.
- Take unclear access to sensitive data.
- Build around vague AI experimentation without a defined operational problem.

## Good-fit projects

A project is a good fit when:

- There is a repeated business workflow causing measurable friction.
- The client can explain who owns the process and what output is needed.
- Inputs, outputs, exceptions, and human approval points can be defined.
- The business wants practical operational improvement rather than novelty.
- Source material and examples can be shared safely.
- The first increment can be scoped small enough to validate.
- The client accepts discovery, audit, validation, handoff, and governance as part of delivery.

## Poor-fit projects

A project is a poor fit when:

- The goal is vague AI adoption without a business workflow.
- The expected outcome depends on replacing staff judgment or accountability.
- Sensitive access is required but governance is unclear.
- The client wants unapproved tools, dependencies, integrations, or infrastructure before discovery.
- The work requires regulated advice outside approved capability.
- The scope is too broad to review safely.
- Success cannot be validated with realistic examples or acceptance criteria.

## Boundary workflow

1. Identify the client request.
2. Map it to a service in `SERVICE_CATALOG.md`.
3. Check fit against this document.
4. Define inputs, outputs, success criteria, and validation using `DELIVERABLE_DEFINITIONS.md`.
5. Stop and ask when a request crosses into pricing, stack, regulated advice, sensitive access, or broad scope.

## Client communication rule

Use plain operational language. Explain what will be reviewed, what will be delivered, how it will be validated, and what is outside scope. Avoid hype and avoid making commitments that require a later technical or commercial decision.
